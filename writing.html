Writing & Insights

I read a lot about how the CS job market has changed: fewer entry-level positions, more competition, AI tools everywhere, and companies optimizing for cost and risk. Instead of ignoring that reality, I try to think clearly about it and adjust my strategy as a student and early-career engineer.

Breaking into CS in a Crowded Market

A few themes keep coming up when I look at discussions from hiring managers, senior engineers, and people who have recently broken in:

  • Real projects beat generic certificates. It’s much more convincing to show a working app, lab, or tool than a list of course names.
  • Domain focus is valuable. Knowing something specific (security, data workflows, backend APIs) helps you stand out compared to being “just another CS student.”
  • Impact matters. Numbers like “17k+ job postings analyzed” or “60% reduction in manual work” are much easier to understand than “I used Python.”
  • Consistency over hype. Long-term effort on a few good projects says more than jumping from trend to trend without depth.

My portfolio reflects this by focusing on fewer, deeper projects (FinTrack, AI JobShield, networked servers, and a security lab) rather than a long list of half-finished experiments.

How This Shapes My Choices

  • Projects with clear users or workflows: personal finance, job seekers, SOC analysts.
  • Stack choices that show fundamentals: React, Django, SQL, Java networking, Python ML.
  • Evidence of impact: automation that saves time, models trained on real datasets, and systems that run reliably.
  • Honesty about where I’m still learning: security lab is a work in progress, and part of its value is documenting that journey.

Over time, I plan to turn these notes into more polished essays and short guides. For now, this page is a snapshot of how I think about career strategy as a Computer Science student approaching graduation and OPT.
